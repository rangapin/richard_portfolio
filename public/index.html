  • Building HackerScope

After resigning from my job and failing a crucial interview, I discovered the transformative power of AI during my post-interview research. This led me to build HackerScope AI—solving my own problem while creating value for others who might face similar challenges.

The Genesis

The choice of Tempo AI felt natural for its integration capabilities. There's a certain poetry in how the idea for HackerScope emerged from a Tempo Labs video showcasing the EXA API—inspiration arriving through the very tools that would build it. Sometimes the universe aligns in unexpected ways.

The Four-Day Journey

Four days. That's all it took, though time moved differently throughout. The first three days felt like weeks—endless loops of errors, particularly the "use client" complications and subscription tier logic that refused to cooperate. Each error message became a lesson in patience, each failed attempt a step toward understanding.

As someone who finds comfort in aesthetic perfection, the design process became its own form of torture. I cycled through countless iterations, each falling short of the vision in my mind. The minimalist approach felt too stark. The playful design seemed unprofessional. Nothing felt quite right, and this paralysis threatened to consume the entire project.

The Breakthrough

Day four arrived with a different energy. Perhaps it was exhaustion, or perhaps clarity, but I approached the work differently. Instead of comprehensive requests, I broke everything into small, precise prompts. Twelve of them, each focused on a single aspect. The code that emerged was clean, purposeful—it had found its voice.

There's something profound about that moment when a project clicks into place. After days of struggle, suddenly the pieces align. The interface felt right. The functionality worked. The vision had materialized, not exactly as imagined, but perhaps better for having survived the journey.

Reflections

Starting over taught me that destruction can be creative. Each restart wasn't a failure but a distillation of learning. The accumulated technical debt of previous attempts fell away, leaving only the essential understanding.

Working with AI requires a different rhythm than traditional development. It demands patience with the process, precision in communication, and acceptance that the path isn't always linear. The tools we use shape not just what we build, but how we think about building.

Perhaps most importantly, I learned that perfection is the enemy of existence. The polished interface I obsessed over mattered less than the functional product that could help someone else. An imperfect solution that exists serves more purpose than a perfect one that remains trapped in endless iteration.

Looking Forward

HackerScope AI now exists in the world—my first real product, born from failure and shaped by persistence. There's a peculiar satisfaction in building a tool I genuinely want to use myself. The AI that helps generate business ideas was itself generated through AI assistance, a recursive loop that feels fitting for our current technological moment.

I find myself eager to see where this leads, both for HackerScope and for whatever ideas it might help uncover. The failure that started this journey has transformed into something beautiful and unexpected—not just a product, but a new way of thinking about creation.

The learning never stops; the building continues.
